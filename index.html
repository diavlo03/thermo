Introduction

This report explores the core concepts of thermodynamics, focusing on heat engines, internal-combustion engines, refrigerators, the second law of thermodynamics, and entropy. These principles are fundamental to understanding how energy is transformed and transferred in physical systems.

Heat Engines, Internal-Combustion Engines, and Refrigerators

Heat Engines

A heat engine is a device that converts thermal energy into mechanical work by exploiting the temperature difference between a hot and a cold reservoir. Classic examples include steam engines and car engines. The engine absorbs heat (\(Q_{in}\)) from the hot reservoir, performs work (\(W\)), and expels some heat (\(Q_{out}\)) to the cold reservoir.

Internal-Combustion Engines

Internal-combustion engines burn fuel in a confined space (the combustion chamber), producing expanding gases that do work on moving parts. Examples include gasoline and diesel engines found in most vehicles.

Refrigerators

Refrigerators work on the reverse principle of heat engines. Instead of converting heat to work, they use external work to transfer heat from a cold space (inside the fridge) to a warmer one (the room), thus keeping the contents cool.

Second Law of Thermodynamics (Law of State of Disorder)

The second law of thermodynamics states that the entropy (a measure of disorder) of an isolated system always increases or remains constant over time; it never decreases. This law explains why processes like mixing, burning fuel, or melting ice are irreversible in nature. In essence, energy spontaneously spreads out if not hindered.

Entropy and Third Law of Thermodynamics (Law of Absolute Zero)

Entropy (\(S\)) increases as energy disperses in a system. When a system reaches absolute zero temperature (0 Kelvin), the third law of thermodynamics states its entropy approaches a minimum value (often zero for a perfect crystal).

Key Formulas

  • Efficiency of a heat engine: \(\eta = \frac{W}{Q_{in}}\)
  • Entropy change: \(\Delta S = \frac{Q}{T}\)
